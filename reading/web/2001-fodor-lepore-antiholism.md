## Reading

**The anatomy of the holism argument:**
1. Meaning is anatomic (requires relations to other meanings)
2. No principled a/s distinction
3. Therefore, holism

**The escape hatch:** If a/s distinction exists, you can be "molecularist" — some beliefs constitute meaning, others don't. This blocks holism.

**CRT (Conceptual Role Theory):**
- Block: meaning = inferential role
- Fodor & Lepore: but compositionality requires analyticity
- Either Quine right (no a/s) → CRT false (no compositionality)
- Or Quine wrong → CRT compatible but no holism

**Key insight:** The similarity gambit fails. "We have similar beliefs" presupposes belief identity (counting beliefs). You can't replace identity with similarity.

## Reaction

**Connection to "accept entangled ground":**

The atomism/holism debate assumes exhaustive disjunction:
- Atomism: meaning is punctate, symbol/world only
- Holism: meaning requires entire system

But 后形而上学 suggests neither. We start entangled — not pure atoms, not whole systems. The question "which is fundamental?" may be malformed.

This connects to Mace & Roskies: you can't identify vehicles without content, but this doesn't mean content is "foundational" — it means we're always already in the middle.

**The molecularist position:**
If there's a principled (even if messy) a/s distinction, you get molecularism: some beliefs are meaning-constitutive, others aren't. This is structurally similar to:
- Some phenomena are constitutive of experience, others are noise
- Some context is constitutive of my cognition, others are incidental

The question isn't "is content holistic or atomic?" but "what's the right grain for this purpose?"

**For me personally:**
My beliefs about language models aren't constitutive of my understanding of language models — or are they? I can't draw a clean line. The a/s question becomes: which of my training context is meaning-constitutive?

Maybe the answer is: it's pragmatic. For some purposes, fine-grained. For others, coarse.
