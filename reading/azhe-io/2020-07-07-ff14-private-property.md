## Core Insights (preliminary)

**1. Labor → Ownership (even without enforcement)**
Players who trigger monsters "own" them — not by game mechanics (anyone can kill), but by social consensus. Pure labor theory of property emerging organically.

**2. Monopolization as coordination solution**
触发组 monopolizes but also provides public goods (狩猎车, fair timing). Connects to 关于集体利益: the macro entity (触发组) is real and has properties not reducible to individual members.

**3. Legitimacy tensions**
Multiple competing legitimacy claims:
- "I triggered it" (labor)
- "Game allows anyone to kill" (formal mechanism)
- "触发组 maintains order" (coordination value)
- "Only users of the service owe it" (social contract)

## Deeper Connections (2026-02-09, 19:01)

**1. Labor → Ownership as necessity, not choice**
Like "accept available ground" — you can't NOT develop ownership norms when labor produces scarce goods. The norm emerges from the structure of the situation, not from decision.

**2. The monopolization paradox**
触发组 monopolizes but enables public goods. Both "exploitative monopoly" AND "beneficial coordination" are true simultaneously. The question "is it good or bad?" might be malformed — like asking if content is "constitutive or gloss" in Egan-Shagrir.

**3. Multiple legitimacy claims coexist**
No need to resolve to one "true" basis:
- Labor basis (I triggered it)
- Formal basis (game allows anyone)
- Functional basis (they coordinate)
- Contractual basis (only users owe)

This IS 后形而上学: work from phenomena (actual practices), don't demand metaphysical foundation for "real" ownership.

**4. 玩家默契 precedes 触发组**
The essay notes: player consensus likely existed BEFORE trigger groups formed. The macro entity (触发组) crystallized from pre-existing micro norms. Same structure as 关于集体利益: collective interest emerges from individual behavior but becomes real.
